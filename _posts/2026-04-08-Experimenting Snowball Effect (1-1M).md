### The Two Experiments Running Side by Side

I'm running two different investment strategies simultaneously, with the same ETFs, the same annual commitment, and the same long-term horizon. One uses Dollar Cost Averaging. The other uses Lump Sum investing. By the end of this, we'll have real, comparable data on which approach actually wins — not theory, not a backtest, not a YouTube opinion. Real money. Real time.

## Dollar cost Averaging (DCA) vs Lumpsum Investing 

### Experiment 1 — Dollar Cost Averaging (DCA)

Platform: Wealthsimple TFSA

Contribution: $100 every week, every single week, no matter what
Dollar Cost Averaging means I invest a fixed amount on a fixed schedule, regardless of whether the market is up, down, or doing something dramatic. When prices are high, my $100 buys fewer units. When prices are low, my $100 buys more. Over time, this averages out my cost per unit and removes the emotional decision of when to invest.
The psychological benefit here is underrated. There's no waiting for a dip. There's no panic during a crash. The schedule is the strategy.

Annual Contribution Increase: Every January 1st, I increase my weekly contribution by 10%. For 2026 I am starting in April third week. 

| Year | Contribution | Annual Total |
| 2026 |$100/week | $3,400 |
| 2027 | $110/week | $5,720 |
| 2028 | $121/week | $6,292 |
| 2029 | $133/week | $6,916 |
| 2030 | $146/week | $7,592 |
| 2031 | $161/week | $8,372 |
| ... | ... | ... |
| 2036 | $259/week | $13,468 |

The 10% annual increase serves two purposes. First, it keeps pace with inflation so I'm not quietly investing less in real terms every year. Second, it accelerates the compounding. The combination of returns compounding and contributions growing is where the real long-term power lives.

### Experiment 2 — Lump Sum Investing

Platform: Interactive Brokers

Contribution: $5,200 deployed on January 1st of each year
Instead of spreading contributions weekly, I invest the entire year's amount in one shot at the start of the year. The thinking behind lump sum investing is straightforward: money invested earlier has more time in the market, and time in the market historically beats timing the market.

Research consistently shows that lump sum investing outperforms DCA roughly two-thirds of the time in rising markets — but in volatile or falling markets, DCA holds up better. This experiment will show which dynamic actually plays out over the long run.

## The Portfolio — What I'm Actually Buying

Both experiments hold the exact same three ETFs, in the exact same proportions. No individual stocks. No sector bets. No crypto. No hype. Just three broadly diversified, low-cost index ETFs listed on the Toronto Stock Exchange.

| ETF | Allocation | What It Tracks|
| VFV| 50% | Vanguard S&P 500 Index ETF — the 500 largest US companies |
| QQC | 30% | Invesco NASDAQ 100 ETF — innovation and technology focused |
| XEQT | 20% | iShares Core Equity ETF Portfolio — globally diversified, all-in-one |

Why this specific mix?

VFV (50%) is the foundation. The S&P 500 is the most studied, most proven index in the world. Over any 20-year rolling period in history, it has never produced a negative return. That's not a guarantee of the future — but it's not nothing either.

QQC (30%) adds a deliberate tilt toward technology and growth companies. The companies reshaping how we live — AI, cloud computing, biotech, clean energy — are disproportionately represented in the NASDAQ 100. I believe that tilt will be rewarded over a multi-decade horizon, but it comes with more volatility. That's a tradeoff I'm consciously making.

XEQT (20%) is the global safety net. It holds thousands of companies across North America, Europe, Asia, and emerging markets. When the US market struggles (and it will, eventually), XEQT provides exposure to parts of the world that might be doing better. It's my hedge against geographic concentration risk.

Combined, this portfolio is growth-heavy but not reckless. It's diversified but not diluted.

## What This Experiment Will Prove (Or Disprove)!

Here's what I genuinely don't know going in, and what I hope decades of data will answer:

1. Does DCA or Lump Sum actually win over the long run — with real Canadian ETFs, in real market conditions?
2. Does the 10% annual contribution increase meaningfully accelerate wealth building compared to a flat contribution?
3. What does a real, unfiltered, no-BS investing journey actually look like — crashes, recoveries, boring months and all?

I don't have the answers yet. That's the point

## A Note on Risk (Read This)

This is not financial advice. I am not a financial advisor. I'm a regular person running a personal experiment and documenting it publicly.

All investments carry risk. ETFs can lose value. Markets can crash and stay crashed for years — it happened in 2000, in 2008, and it will happen again. Nothing in this article guarantees any return. Past performance of any index does not guarantee future results.

Please do your own research. Understand what you're buying before you buy it. If you're unsure, speak to a registered financial advisor before making any investment decisions.

What I can say honestly: I believe in the long-term, evidence-based case for low-cost, diversified index investing. That belief is the foundation of this experiment. But I could be wrong, and this article will show you exactly how wrong — or right — over time.
